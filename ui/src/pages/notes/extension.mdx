I wouldn't say I have an addictive personality, but the seemingly educational content on
Youbute certainly hits a soft spot in my brain. Too broad the scope, from woodworking and
lathe work to photography and physics, too nicely packaged, fast-paced and information
packed, for me to resist. The content is designed to attract engagement after all. 
This is to say if I have the choice of what to do at home, I'm on Youtube entirely 
to often. It's devious in that it's hard to look back on 2 hours spent without having
learned a bunch of stuff (most of it is useless but I digress). In that way it keeps me
trapped, it's not like I'm wasting my time here, right?

I've been aware of this pattern for years, but didn't do anything about it. Then, I installed
[LeechBlock](https://addons.mozilla.org/en-US/firefox/addon/leechblock-ng/), a Firefox extension
that allows you to set up block rules for certain websites. Like say, at most 30 of Youtube per
day and after that it will block further access. I overruled the block entirely too often anyway.

Then, a couple of weeks ago I had an idea. What if I don't just allow myself a maximum 
duration of Youtube per day that resets every day but instead kept a rolling balance 
that increases by X minutes every day and decreases by however much I use of it. 
Effectively allowing a roll over of unused time.
I postulated that this may help pyschologicaly as not spending time on Youtube today is not
lost opportunity, but rather delayed gratification.

While LeechBlock has the option to roll-over time, it doesn't quite work the way I need
it to. So I set out to build my own.

Starting out with a tutorial on MDN, I was surprised how easy it is to get a first demo
extension built and installed. From there, I was iterating with Claude to get the behaviour
I need. And boy did it take some prompting. While Claude understands the APIs available
to an extension just fine, it struggled with concepts like timers not firing when the
device is sleeping etc.

The result is this [tiny extension](https://github.com/beingflo/block-extension). It
keeps track of the last time budget and when it was updated in local storage. Whenever
Youtube is in focus, it updates the budget and update timestamp every second.
When there is a visibility change, it checks when the budget was last updated and
adds time as appropriate. I currently have it afford 2.5 minutes every hour, this comes to
1 hour of Youtube per day. You gotta start somewhere.

It worked well so far. As theorized, I have an easier time doing something else and
not feeling like I'm missing out, as I'm just _postponing_ watching that video where
the guy explains how dishwashers work.
